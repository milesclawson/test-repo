Our Own Nation

A story with Miigizi and Winona

One morning, Miigizi's teacher asked the class: "What is a government?"

"It's the people who make rules," said one student.

"It's who you vote for," said another.

Miigizi raised his hand. "My nation has its own government," he said. "White Earth has its own president — we call him the Tribal Chairman. And its own laws. And its own court."

His teacher nodded. "That's called sovereignty," she said. "Tribal nations in Minnesota have their own governments. They always have."

At lunch, Miigizi told Winona what happened.

"My dad says sovereignty means we take care of ourselves," Winona said. "Shakopee has its own fire department, its own school, its own clinic. If someone in our community needs help — we help them."

"It's like being a country," Miigizi said.

"Kind of," Winona said. "A country inside a country. With our own flag. Our own laws. Our own people making decisions for our people."

She paused. "And our own language. That's important too."

After school, Miigizi's dad took him to a community meeting at the White Earth tribal offices in Minneapolis.

Adults sat in a circle, talking about a new community garden. One person suggested a location. Another asked about water access. A grandmother spoke about traditional foods they could grow. Everyone listened.

"Why does everyone get to talk?" Miigizi whispered.

"That's how we make decisions," his dad whispered back. "Not one person — the community together. That's been our way for a long time."

On the drive home, Miigizi looked out the window at the city lights.

"There are eleven tribal nations in Minnesota," his dad said. "Some people don't know that. They think Native people are only in history books."

"We're not history," Miigizi said. He had heard Winona say that.

"No," his dad said. "We are governments. We are schools. We are language programs and health clinics and fire departments and environmental teams. We are present."

He turned on the radio. A song in Ojibwemowin came on. Miigizi smiled.

Then & Now: Tribal Sovereignty

📜 Always Sovereign

Tribal nations governed themselves long before European contact. They had their own laws, leaders, trade networks, and ways of making decisions. Sovereignty is not something they were given — it is something they always had.

Treaties recognized this sovereignty by treating tribal nations as governments equal to the US government at the negotiating table.

🏛️ Sovereign Today

Today, Minnesota's 11 tribal nations run their own schools, health clinics, courts, environmental departments, fire departments, and cultural programs. They pass their own laws — like the Rights of Manoomin.

Sovereignty means the power to take care of your own people, in your own way, on your own land.

📚 Sources MDE IEFA — Tribal Relations Training IEFA Presentation (2024)  ·  MDE — Lower Sioux Caŋṡayapi curriculum  ·  Minnesota Statutes, section 120B.021, subdivision 5

💬 Discussion Questions

  1. Miigizi says a tribal nation is "like a country inside a country." What does that mean? Can you explain it in your own words?
  2. At the community meeting, everyone got to speak before a decision was made. How is that similar to or different from how decisions are made at your school?
  3. There are 11 tribal nations in Minnesota. Did you know that before today? Why might it be important to know?
  4. Sovereignty means taking care of your own people. What do you think communities need to do to take care of each other?
