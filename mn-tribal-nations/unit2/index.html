Where the Water Reflects the Sky

A story with Winona  ·  Tap words in red to hear their meaning

Winona's favorite drive was south on the highway, past the cornfields, past the river bluffs, until her dad turned off toward Šakpe Otonwe.

"Mni Sota Makoce," her grandmother said from the back seat. "That's what Dakota people call this place. Not Minnesota — Mni Sota Makoce. Our name. Our land."

Winona pressed her face to the window. The river was wide and silver in the morning light. The water was reflecting the sky — just like the name said.

At her grandmother's house, Winona's Uŋčí was already cooking. The whole house smelled like soup and frybread.

"Táku eháŋni yaúŋ he?" her Uŋčí called. What have you been doing?

"School," Winona said. "We're learning about Dakota people. I told my teacher — we're not in the past. We're right here."

Her Uŋčí smiled. "You tell them right."

After lunch, Winona's dad spread a big map on the kitchen table. It showed Minnesota — but labeled with Dakota words.

"Bdote," her dad said, pointing to where two rivers met. "The center of the Dakota world. Where we come from. It's still there — right in the middle of the Twin Cities."

"People drive past it every day," Winona said quietly.

"They do. But we know what it is." He smiled. "And now you do too."

That evening, Winona's cousin showed her the tribal community center — a big, beautiful building with a garden outside.

"The tribe built this," her cousin said proudly. "We have our own school, our own clinic, our own fire department. We make our own decisions."

Winona thought about what her teacher had said about the past — about hard times, about land taken away. But standing here, she felt something different. She felt wóčhaŋtognake — the generous, open heart of a community that takes care of its own.

Dakota people were not just history. They were here. Building. Growing. Thriving.

Then & Now: Dakota People in Minnesota

🌊 Then — Thousands of Years

Dakota peoples have lived in Mni Sota Makoce since time immemorial. The land, rivers, and lakes all carry Dakota names. Bdote — where the Minnesota and Mississippi rivers meet — is the center of the Dakota world.

Dakota communities lived, hunted, gathered, farmed, and built relationships across the region. Their knowledge of this land is deep and old.

🏛️ Now — Four Communities, Strong and Growing

Today there are four federally recognized Dakota communities in Minnesota: Lower Sioux, Upper Sioux, Prairie Island, and Shakopee Mdewakanton. Each runs its own government, schools, health programs, and cultural initiatives.

Shakopee Mdewakanton runs one of the most successful tribal economic programs in the country — funding language revitalization, education, and community services.

💬 Discussion Questions

  1. Winona tells her teacher: "We're not in the past. We're right here." What do you think she means? Why is it important to say that?
  2. Bdote is described as the center of the Dakota world. What place feels like the center of your world? What makes somewhere feel important like that?
  3. Minnesota's name comes from Dakota words. Can you find other places near your school or home that have Dakota or Ojibwe names?
  4. Winona feels wóčhaŋtognake — the warmth of a community that takes care of its own. What does that feel like? What makes a community strong?
