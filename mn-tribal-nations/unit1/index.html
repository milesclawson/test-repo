The Gift That Grows in Water

A story with Miigizi and Winona  ·  Tap words in green or red to hear their meaning

It was late summer, and Miigizi was excited. Tomorrow, he and his Nokomis would drive north from Minneapolis to meet family on the water.

"We're going to harvest manoomin," he told his friend Winona at school. "My Nokomis says the Anishinaabe people have harvested it for thousands of years."

Winona smiled. "My dad talks about psin too," she said. "That's what we call it in Dakota Iapi. My kokáku — my grandpa — says it's a gift from the water."

The next morning, Miigizi and his Nokomis drove through tall pines to reach a wide, shallow lake. His aunties and cousins were already there, loading into flat-bottomed canoes.

"The manoomin plants grow tall from the water," his Nokomis said, handing him two smooth wooden sticks. "We use these bizhikiins — the knocking sticks — to tap the stalks gently. The ripe rice falls into the canoe. We never take all of it. We leave some behind so it grows again."

Miigizi listened carefully. "Why do we leave some?" he asked.

"Because manoomin is a relative," she said. "Not just food. A relative."

Meanwhile, back in the Twin Cities, Winona's dad came home with exciting news.

"The White Earth Nation," he said, "just passed a law giving manoomin its own legal rights. Wild rice now has the right to exist. To be healthy. To keep growing."

Winona's eyes went wide. "Can a plant have rights?"

"Tribal nations can make their own laws," her dad said. "That's what sovereignty means. Our nations have always known that the land and water are alive. Now we're writing that into law."

That night, Miigizi helped his Nokomis parch the rice over a fire — carefully stirring it so it dried just right. The smell was warm and nutty and good.

"Miigwech, manoomin," his Nokomis whispered to the rice. Thank you.

Miigizi said it too, quietly. Miigwech.

He thought about what Winona's dad had said. Wild rice with its own rights. A plant that was a relative. A nation that could make laws to protect something it loved.

He didn't have words for everything he felt. But it felt important. It felt like something worth learning more about.

Seed Sinks
Biidaasamose

Ripe seeds fall into the water and sink to the lake bottom in fall.

❄️
Winter Rest
Biboon — Waníyetu

Seeds rest under ice and cold water all winter long.

☀️
Spring Sprout
Ziigwan — Wetu

When water warms, seeds sprout and grow up toward sunlight.

🌿
Plant Grows
Ode'imini-giizis

Plants grow tall — up to 8 feet — in 1–3 feet of water.

🌾
Rice Ripens
Manoominike-giizis

Late August–September: harvest time. "Manoomin moon."

🛶
Harvest
Manoominike

Harvesters use canoes and knocking sticks. Some seeds are left to regrow.

🔥
Parching
Iskigamizigan

Rice is dried over fire to loosen the hulls and bring out the flavor.

🌱
Seeds Return
Biidaasamose

Left-behind seeds sink and the cycle begins again. The relationship continues.

Then & Now: Manoomin Through Time

🌾 Then — Thousands of Years

Anishinaabe and Dakota peoples have harvested wild rice from Minnesota's lakes for thousands of years. It is a central food, a cultural teaching, and a spiritual relationship — not just a crop.

Harvesting was done in canoes using knocking sticks. Communities gathered together. Rice was parched, stored, and shared through the winter.

🏛️ Now — Sovereignty in Action

In 2021, the White Earth Band of Ojibwe passed the Rights of Manoomin — the first law in the US to give wild rice its own legal rights to exist and thrive.

Today, tribal nations monitor water quality, work with scientists, and protect rice beds from pollution and invasive species. Harvesting continues. The relationship continues.

📚 Then & Now Sources Historical context from MDE Indigenous Education for All curriculum materials, including the Fond du Lac Multimedia Gallery. Contemporary sovereignty information from White Earth Nation public records, referenced in MDE's IEFA resources. Learn more at education.mn.gov/MDE/dse/indian/all.

🏛️ Big Idea: What Is Sovereignty?

Minnesota's 11 tribal nations are sovereign nations — meaning they have their own governments, pass their own laws, and make their own decisions. They are not part of Minnesota's state government.

The Rights of Manoomin law is an example of sovereignty in action. The White Earth Nation decided that wild rice deserved legal protection — and they had the power to make that law themselves.

"A tribal nation is like a country within a country. It has its own flag, its own courts, its own schools — and the right to protect the things that matter most to its people."

💬 Discussion Questions

  1. Miigizi's Nokomis says manoomin is a "relative, not just food." What do you think she means? How is that different from how you think about food?
  2. Winona's dad explains that the White Earth Nation gave wild rice its own legal rights. Why might a community want to protect something they love by making a law about it?
  3. When harvesting, Miigizi is taught to leave some rice behind. Can you think of other ways people take care of something they love by not taking too much?
  4. What does sovereignty mean? Can you explain it to a friend using the manoomin story as an example?
