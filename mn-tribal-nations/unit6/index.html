Names Remember

A story with Miigizi and Winona

Miigizi's class had a project: find the original name of a place near your home.

He typed his street address into a search and found something surprising. The neighborhood he lived in — just south of downtown Minneapolis — sat on land near Bde Maka Ska.

"That's the Dakota name," Winona told him the next day at school. "My dad told me. It means White Earth Lake. They changed it back a few years ago — the city voted to use the original name."

Miigizi felt something warm about that. "So the name was there this whole time. We just stopped using it for a while."

"Names remember," Winona said. "Even when people forget."

In social studies, they learned about treaties. A treaty is a formal agreement between two nations — like a country-to-country promise.

"The United States and the tribal nations signed treaties," their teacher said. "Tribal nations agreed to share land. In return, the US government promised certain rights — like the right to fish, hunt, and gather in their traditional territories, forever."

"Did they keep the promises?" Miigizi asked.

His teacher paused. "Not always. That's a hard and true thing. But tribal nations have fought in courts to protect their treaty rights — and they've won important cases."

"So the promises are still real?" Winona asked.

"Yes," her teacher said. "Treaties are still the law."

For his project, Miigizi found the Ojibwe name for a lake near his grandparents' home up north.

The lake was called Leech Lake on maps. But its Ojibwe name was Miskwaaziibiing — place of red pike.

"That's a better name," he said. "It tells you something about the place."

He looked up more names. Gaa-miskwaawaakokaag — Red Lake. Gaa-wiindamaagozid — Fond du Lac. Each name was a description. Each one carried knowledge of the land.

Winona's project focused on a place near Shakopee.

She found that the Minnesota River had been called Mní Sóta Wakpá — sky-tinted water river — long before anyone called it the Minnesota River.

"Minnesota comes from our word," she wrote in her report. "This state was named after something Dakota people noticed about the water. We named it first."

At the end of the project, the class made a map — their town, with both names for every place they could find. Old name and new name, together.

It felt like meeting the land for the first time. Even though they'd lived here all along.

What Is a Treaty?

Promises between nations — and why they still matter today  ·  Source: MDE IEFA

A treaty is an official agreement between two or more nations — like a country-to-country contract. Tribal nations in Minnesota signed several important treaties with the US government. These treaties are still legal documents today.

1837

Treaty of St. Peters

Anishinaabe nations ceded (shared) land but kept the right to hunt, fish, and gather in the ceded territory "during the pleasure of the President." Tribal nations fought to keep these rights — and won key court cases confirming them.

1851

Treaties of Traverse des Sioux & Mendota

Dakota nations signed treaties giving up large amounts of land. The promises made in return were often broken. But the treaties also confirmed Dakota presence and rights in Minnesota.

1854–1867

Later Treaties

Additional treaties established reservation boundaries for Ojibwe bands in Minnesota, including Fond du Lac, Grand Portage, Mille Lacs, and others. Tribal nations continued to assert and defend their treaty rights in courts.

Today

Treaties Are Still Law

The US Constitution says treaties are "the supreme law of the land." Minnesota tribal nations continue to exercise treaty rights — including rights to fish, hunt, and gather — protected by these agreements. Treaties are present, not past.

🗺️ Student Activity: Map Your Neighborhood

  1. Write down the names of three places near your school or home — a lake, a river, a park, or a street.
  2. Use the Fond du Lac Roads & Buildings resource and Dakhota.org to look up whether any have Ojibwe or Dakota names.
  3. Draw a small map showing your place — write both names if you find one. If you can't find the original name, write a question mark and make a note to investigate more.
  4. Share with the class: what surprised you? What does the original name tell you about what the place used to be like?

Then & Now: Place Names & Promises

📍 Then — Names That Described the Land

Dakota and Ojibwe place names describe what the land looks, sounds, and feels like — the quality of the water, the animals present, the shape of the shore. They encode ecological knowledge in language.

When these names were replaced with English names, that knowledge was hidden — but not destroyed. The original names were preserved in communities, in documents, and in memory.

🗺️ Now — Names Coming Back

In 2018, Minneapolis officially restored the name Bde Maka Ska (White Earth Lake) to what had been called Lake Calhoun. This is one example of place name restoration happening across Minnesota and the country.

The Fond du Lac Band has documented Ojibwe names for roads, buildings, and waterways — kept alive for students and communities. That work is linked from MDE IEFA.

💬 Discussion Questions

  1. Winona says "names remember, even when people forget." What do you think place names can remember that history books might miss?
  2. A treaty is a promise between nations. What makes a promise official? What should happen when a promise is broken?
  3. If you could restore one place name near your school to its original language, which would you choose? What would the new/old name tell people?
  4. Miigizi's class made a map with two names for every place. Why might it be valuable to show both names?
