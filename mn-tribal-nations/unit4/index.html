The Year Miigizi Paid Attention

A story with Miigizi and Winona  ·  Tap green or red words to learn more

Miigizi's Nokomis had a rule: no phones outside from ziigwan to dagwaagin.

"You have to pay attention to the world," she said. "The land is always teaching. But only if you're quiet enough to listen."

Miigizi thought that sounded boring. But he put his phone away.

The first thing he noticed was the smell — warm earth and something green and alive. Spring was here. And something was waking up.

In ziigwan, Nokomis tapped the maple tree in the yard. Sap ran out into a bucket — clear and barely sweet.

"We boil it down," she said. "Forty gallons of sap makes one gallon of syrup. The tree gives us a gift. We take care of the tree."

Miigizi tasted some. It was light and good.

"The Fond du Lac Band has videos of elders doing this," his Nokomis said. "With Ojibwe words. You should watch them."

He did. He watched them that night, his Nokomis sitting beside him, correcting his pronunciation, laughing at his mistakes — in a kind way.

By niibino-giizis — the summer moon — Winona and her dad were monitoring a creek near their house.

"The tribe's environmental team comes here," her dad said. "They test the water. They count the fish. They make sure the watershed is healthy."

"Like scientists?" Winona asked.

"Like scientists and like stewards," he said. Makoce — the land — needed to be cared for. Not just used.

When manoominike-giizis rose, Miigizi went north to harvest rice again. He was getting good with the knocking sticks. He left some behind, like always.

In biboon, the family played games inside — the Moccasin Game, the Bowl Game. Nokomis told stories. The land rested. The family rested.

"The land teaches you to rest too," Nokomis said. "That is also traditional knowledge."

Miigizi looked at his phone — then put it down. He was listening.

Then & Now: Traditional Ecological Knowledge

🌱 Then — Thousands of Years of Stewardship

Anishinaabe and Dakota peoples developed deep ecological knowledge over thousands of years — when to harvest, how much to take, how to care for the land so it gives again next year.

This wasn't just survival — it was a relationship. The land, water, plants, and animals were relatives, not resources.

🔬 Now — Science and Traditional Knowledge Together

Today, the Fond du Lac Band combines traditional ecological knowledge with western science. Their environmental department monitors water quality, manages forests, and protects wild rice — using both approaches together.

This "two-eyed seeing" approach is used by tribal environmental departments across Minnesota.

📚 Sources Fond du Lac Multimedia Gallery (Hamline University) via MDE IEFA  ·  MIIN 3rd Grade — Bdote and Niigaane science curricula

💬 Discussion Questions

  1. Miigizi's Nokomis says the land is "always teaching." What does she mean? What might the land teach that a book can't?
  2. Anishinaabe people name their months by what is happening in nature — the Wild Rice Moon, the Blueberry Moon. What would you name the moon for the time of year we're in right now?
  3. Winona's dad says the tribe's environmental team are "scientists and stewards." What is the difference? Can you be both at once?
  4. The story says "the land teaches you to rest too." Why might rest be important — for people and for the land?
