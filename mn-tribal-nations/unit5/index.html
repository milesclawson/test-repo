The Words That Were Almost Lost

A story with Miigizi and Winona

Miigizi's Nokomis didn't speak much Ojibwe. She knew some words — miigwech, boozhoo, aaniish. But she had not grown up speaking it fully.

"Why not?" Miigizi asked one evening.

His Nokomis was quiet for a moment. "When I was young," she said, "there were schools that punished children for speaking their language. Our words were taken from us."

Miigizi felt something heavy in his chest. "That doesn't seem right."

"It wasn't," she said. "But here's what matters now: people are bringing it back. That's what MIIN does. That's what your cousin at language camp is doing. That's what you are doing — right now — when you say boozhoo instead of just hello."

Winona had a different experience. Her dad had grown up hearing Dakota Iapi at home. Not perfectly — but enough that words appeared naturally in his sentences.

"Čhaŋtéčhiŋza, Winona," he would say when she tried something hard. Strong-hearted.

At school, she was starting a language unit with her class. They were learning to say hello in five languages. She added a sixth: "Haŋ."

Her teacher asked what it meant. Winona smiled. "It's Dakota. My language."

Miigizi signed up for the MIIN after-school language program. The first day, he felt nervous. Would he be bad at it? Would the other kids already know more than him?

The teacher — a young woman from Mille Lacs — started with a song. Everyone sang together, even the kids who didn't know all the words.

"Awenen giin?" the teacher sang. Who are you?

"Awenen niin," the class sang back. I am...

And then each person said their name. Miigizi said his. It sounded right in Ojibwemowin. It had always been an Ojibwe word — he just hadn't known it until now.

After class, Miigizi texted Winona: "I learned my name means Eagle. I already knew that. But hearing it in Ojibwe is different."

Winona texted back: "I know. Some things only work in the right language."

She was practicing too — using the Dakhota.org website, the Story Dice game her dad had bought, the Dakota language cards. Each word felt like finding something that had always belonged to her.

Languages don't die easily, Miigizi was learning. And they don't come back easily either. But they do come back — when people like his Nokomis tell the truth, and people like him decide to listen.

Language Revitalization — Then & Now

These languages are alive, growing, and being reclaimed  ·  Source: MIIN & Dakhota.org

🌿 MIIN — Midwest Indigenous Immersion Network

MIIN supports Ojibwemowin immersion education across Minnesota. Their website (miinojibwe.org) offers free curriculum for every grade — Birth through Adult. The free MIIN Ojibwe app delivers an Ojibwe Word of the Day on any phone or tablet.

This work matters because Ojibwemowin is a language still being revitalized by living communities — not a historical artifact.

🌻 Dakhota.org — Dakota Language & Culture

Dakhota.org offers the Dakhóta Iá Wóhdaka Po! ("Speak Dakota!") textbook series, the Wóeye Káǧapi Story Dice game, the Dakota Language Reader, and free keyboard tools for writing in Dakota.

Their 2026 Dakota Language Calendar connects language learning to every month of the year. Their work is centered in Dakota communities' own language reclamation efforts.

📚 GLIFWC INWE Storybooks — Free on MIIN

The Great Lakes Indian Fish & Wildlife Commission (GLIFWC) has published the Nenda-gikendamang Ningo-biboonagak storybook series — PDFs with accompanying activities and workbooks — free at the MIIN 3rd Grade page.

🎨 Art Activity: My Language Card

Students create a personal "language card" — a small decorated card that shows their name written out phonetically in Ojibwe or Dakota sounds, plus one word in each language that means something to them.

Steps: (1) Choose a name from the Ojibwe or Dakota language or write your English name phonetically. (2) Decorate with patterns inspired by the Fond du Lac beadwork coloring book. (3) Write one Ojibwe word and one Dakota word you want to remember. (4) Share with the class — pronounce your card together.

Beadwork coloring book: free at Fond du Lac Ojibwemowin Learning Materials via MDE IEFA.

💬 Discussion Questions

  1. Winona says "some things only work in the right language." What do you think she means? Can you think of a word or phrase in any language that doesn't translate perfectly?
  2. Miigizi's Nokomis says that when he says "boozhoo" instead of "hello," he is helping bring the language back. How can small choices matter in a big way?
  3. Why is a language more than just words? What would be lost if a language disappeared?
  4. MIIN and Dakhota.org are organizations working to keep languages alive. If you were going to keep something important alive for future kids — what would it be and how would you do it?
